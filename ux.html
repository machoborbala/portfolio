TDK project
User-centered analysis of banking mobile apps using qualitative and quantitative methods

Download the full esse

In 2022, I was introduced to the basics of UI and UX design within the framework of software ergonomics. Together with a group mate, we chose a research project announced by the Hungarian National Bank for our course project, and later presented it at the Scientific Students' Associations Conference (TDK), where we achieved second place in our section, and at the National Association for Student Research (OTDK), where we were awarded first place. The aim of our research was to compare and analyze current mobile banking applications using a combination of qualitative and quantitative methods. We combined analytical and empirical approaches to assess the user experience. A usability study was conducted with the support of Tobii Nano eye-tracking technology, and participants also completed a System Usability Scale (SUS) assessment. These findings were further enriched through a series of expert interviews with professionals from the financial technology (FinTech) sector, along with a large-scale user questionnaire. Based on the insights gathered, we formulated development recommendations for both the user interface and core functionalities of an ideal mobile banking application, and created a clickable prototype to demonstrate its key features.

Role

This was a TDK project completed with another student, under the guidance of a supervisor, focusing on user-centered research and design.

Impact

Our findings led to the design of a prototype banking app with a clean, intuitive interface, featuring quick balance check and easy transfers. The thesis received second place in its TDK section and first place at the OTDK.

Context

We analyzed banking applications using qualitative and quantitative methods for a Hungarian National Bank grant, identifying key user needs and pain points. The study was later presented as a TDK project.

Timeline

Research: 2022 Q3 – Q4
Thesis writing: 2023 Q1 – Q2

Research process

The aim of our research was to explore user opinions on the mobile applications of domestic banks and to design an ideal user interface based on these insights. We began by outlining the research framework and selecting appropriate methodologies. The chosen methods included: an eye-tracking usability study, the System Usability Scale (SUS), follow-up user interviews, a series of expert interviews, and a large-sample questionnaire.

During the usability study, we employed Tobii Nano eye-tracking technology to evaluate the mobile applications of seven different banks. Each of the seven participants tested five applications, resulting in a total of 35 individual tests. Participants were instructed to verbalize their thoughts while completing specific tasks (think-aloud protocol). The collected data was visualized through heatmaps and gaze plots, allowing us to analyze attention distribution and navigation patterns.

Following the usability tests, participants completed the System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire to evaluate the perceived usability of each application. The SUS consisted of 10 standardized items rated on a 5-point Likert scale. Results indicated that OTP Bank and Gránit Bank received the highest usability scores. Applications were rated more favorably when they featured a visually appealing design, clear categorization, intuitive navigation, and well-labeled icons. Conversely, lower scores were associated with cluttered layouts, slow loading times, poor navigation structures, and unnecessary procedural steps.

We conducted in-depth interviews with five industry experts. The interviews explored current development trends, common pitfalls in banking app design, the characteristics of an ideal banking application, and future directions for digital banking. Key findings highlighted the importance of streamlining basic operations — such as initiating transfers, checking balances, and reviewing transaction history. Additional priorities included technological reliability, personalization, a diverse range of digital services, and ensuring accessibility for all users.

Our online questionnaire was completed by over 260 respondents. The questionnaire began with demographic data collection, followed by questions addressing general banking habits, financial literacy, and preferred platforms for conducting banking activities. Participants also provided feedback on their current mobile banking experiences and their views on neobanks. Key findings highlighted the importance of simplicity, ease of access, transparency, and convenience. There was also a clear demand for features such as spending categorization, graphical visualizations, integration of all banking functionalities, international transfers, detailed account history, and the ability to purchase securities and government bonds directly through domestic banking apps.

Results and prototyping

At the conclusion of the research, we translated our findings into a conceptual design by developing several key screens of an ideal banking application and creating a clickable prototype. The most frequently used functions were prioritized and placed on the home screen, alongside a spending chart to enhance financial transparency.
To support financial literacy, we introduced a financial assistant concept, designed to help users better understand and manage their personal finances. Based on our insights, we recommended that banks reassess their IT infrastructure and optimize their banking processes with a user-centered approach, focusing on accessibility, clarity, and seamless user experience.
